How Much Do Airbnb Hosts Actually Keep?

The full fee picture — what Airbnb takes, what operating costs eat, and what's really left after a month of hosting.

By Gia Gray  ·  Last updated May 2026  ·  9 min read

The Gap Between Gross and Net

New hosts almost always focus on the gross revenue number — $150/night at 60% occupancy is $2,700/month! — and work backward from there to decide if Airbnb is worth it.

The problem is that $2,700 is not what lands in your bank account. By the time you subtract Airbnb's cut, cleaning costs, supplies, utilities, and a maintenance reserve, the real number is typically 50–65% of gross. Sometimes less.

This guide walks through every deduction so you know what to expect.

Step 1: Airbnb's Host Fee (3%)

Under Airbnb's default split-fee model, hosts pay about 3% of the booking subtotal. That's the nightly rate times number of nights, plus any cleaning fee you charge.

On a $150/night, 3-night booking with a $75 cleaning fee, the subtotal is $525. Airbnb takes $15.75 (3%). You receive $509.25 from that booking.

It sounds small — and individually it is. But across a full month of bookings, it adds up to $60–$90 on a typical one-bedroom listing.

Step 2: Cleaning Costs

This is where hosts bleed money without realizing it. Every guest turnover costs money — either your time or a cleaner's rate.

  • Professional cleaner for a 1-bedroom: $80–$150 per turnover
  • At 60% occupancy with 2-night average stays: roughly 9 turnovers/month
  • Monthly cleaning cost at $100/clean: $900/month

Many hosts charge guests a cleaning fee to offset this. A $75 cleaning fee on a 2-night stay covers most of the cost — but on a 1-night stay, you're taking in $75 and spending $100 or more to clean. Short stays are often net-negative on cleaning alone.

This is why minimum night requirements matter. A 2-night minimum cuts your turnovers roughly in half compared to no minimum.

Step 3: Supplies and Consumables

Every guest uses something: toilet paper, hand soap, dish soap, coffee pods, trash bags, paper towels. Add in laundry costs (washing linens after every turnover) and it runs $30–$80 a month depending on your turnover frequency and what you provide.

It's a small line item but it's a guaranteed one. Budget $50/month for a one-bedroom and you'll be close.

Step 4: Utilities

Unlike long-term rentals where the tenant pays utilities, Airbnb hosts almost always cover electricity, water, gas, WiFi, and streaming. Guests use these freely — they have no incentive not to.

Utility costs for a one-bedroom apartment typically run $150–$250/month. Some hosts in hot or cold climates pay more because guests crank the A/C or heat and leave it running when they go out.

Step 5: Maintenance Reserve

Things break. A guest breaks a wine glass (common). The shower drain clogs (very common). The sofa gets a stain. The TV remote stops working. The microwave dies.

A standard rule in real estate is to reserve 1% of property value per year for maintenance. For an Airbnb, 1.5–2% is more realistic because STR properties see higher wear. On a $300,000 property, that's $375–$500/month set aside.

Most hosts ignore this until something expensive breaks. Don't. Set $150–$300/month aside as a reserve — you'll need it eventually.

Step 6: Insurance

Airbnb's AirCover program provides some host protection, but it has exclusions, claim limits, and a review process that can take weeks. Most experienced hosts carry a dedicated short-term rental insurance policy in addition to or instead of relying on AirCover.

STR-specific policies (from companies like Proper Insurance or Steadily) typically run $1,200–$3,000/year depending on property value and location. That's $100–$250/month.

The Full Math: What You Actually Keep

Let's run the numbers for a one-bedroom in a mid-size U.S. city. Nightly rate: $130. Occupancy: 62% (about 19 nights/month). Average stay: 2.5 nights.

Line ItemMonthly Amount
Gross booking revenue (19 nights × $130)$2,470
Airbnb host fee (3%)−$74
Cleaning costs (~8 turnovers × $100)−$800
Supplies and consumables−$50
Utilities−$180
Maintenance reserve−$200
Insurance (STR policy)−$150
Net operating income$1,016

That's 41% of gross revenue — before property costs (mortgage or rent). If this host has a $1,500/month mortgage, they're net-negative. At $800/month mortgage, they're netting $216/month — not much for the effort involved.

The real margin: In a typical U.S. market, most active Airbnb hosts keep 40–60% of their gross booking revenue after operating costs. After property costs (mortgage), the actual profit margin is often much thinner than the gross number suggests.

When the Numbers Work

The math works best when one or more of these is true:

  • Your nightly rate is high relative to costs — vacation markets with $300+/night rates absorb the same fixed costs much more easily
  • You own the property outright — no mortgage payment changes everything
  • Your cleaning costs are low — you clean yourself, or you have a low-cost cleaner, or you enforce longer minimum stays to reduce turnovers
  • Occupancy is consistently above 65% — the fixed costs are spread across more revenue nights

What You Can Control

You can't change Airbnb's fee. You can't make utilities cheaper. But you can control:

  • Minimum night requirement — raising from 1 to 2 nights cuts turnovers and cleaning costs roughly in half
  • Nightly rate — even a $10–$15 increase compresses your cost ratio significantly at the same occupancy
  • Cleaning fee structure — charge a cleaning fee that actually covers your cleaning cost, not a round number you made up
  • Supplier costs — bulk buying toilet paper, soap, and consumables from Costco cuts supply costs by 30–40%
